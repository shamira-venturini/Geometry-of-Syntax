\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Corpora and provenance}

We analyzed the transitive active/passive alternation only. Dative materials were
not included in this draft.

The \texttt{CORE} and \texttt{ANOMALOUS} corpora come from the Prime-LM line of work
(Sinclair et al., 2022) and were used here via:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \texttt{corpora/transitive/CORE\_transitive\_15000sampled\_10-1.csv}
    \item \texttt{corpora/transitive/ANOMALOUS\_chomsky\_transitive\_15000sampled\_10-1.csv}
\end{itemize}

For \texttt{CORE}, Sinclair et al.\ describe the materials as
``designed to control for lexical overlap and semantic similarity''
(2022, p.~1037). For the implausibility manipulation corresponding to our
\texttt{ANOMALOUS} condition, they state that
``prime sentences are specifically designed to be semantically implausible''
(2022, p.~1039).

The third condition is a strict Jabberwocky transitive corpus:
\texttt{corpora/transitive/jabberwocky\_transitive\_bpe\_filtered.csv}.
Its design goal is lexical content degradation with English morphosyntax
preserved. Function words, auxiliaries, inflectional morphology, and
argument-structure templates were retained, while lexical-semantic anchoring of
content words was minimized.

\subsection*{Jabberwocky norming and control studies}

Three norming/control passes were performed in this repository before the final
Jabberwocky corpus was fixed.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Lexical audit
    (\texttt{scripts/Phase-1/1\_audit\_jabberwocky\_lexicon.py}): the final
    nonce inventory contains no exact English matches, no overlap with the
    Prime-LM vocabulary list, and no close-form neighbors under the screening
    criteria used here.
    \item Semantic audit
    (\texttt{scripts/Phase-1/2\_audit\_jabberwocky\_semantics.py}): the revised
    inventory showed reduced lexical-semantic anchoring relative to the legacy
    Jabberwocky materials, especially for nouns and past-tense verb forms.
    \item Tokenizer-length filter
    (\texttt{scripts/Phase-1/4\_filter\_jabberwocky\_tokenizer\_lengths.py}):
    noun forms and verb present/past forms were filtered to stable GPT-2 BPE
    lengths (4--5 tokens) to reduce extreme segmentation imbalance in the final
    corpus.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Models}

The first full run used \texttt{gpt2-large}. We then repeated the same
experiment with \texttt{EleutherAI/pythia-410m}.

\texttt{pythia-410m} was selected as the replication model for two reasons.
First, it is a decoder-only causal language model with standard left-to-right
generation, making it directly comparable to \texttt{gpt2-large} for the same
teacher-forced priming assay. Second, it is much more suitable for the planned
follow-up mechanistic-interpretability/editing work: it is smaller, faster to
run repeatedly, and commonly used in open-weight interpretability pipelines.
The choice was therefore not meant to maximize raw performance, but to provide a
clean behavioral replication in a model family that is also practical for
subsequent causal intervention studies.

\subsection*{Priming computation}

Both models were run with
\texttt{scripts/Phase-1/2\_transitive\_token\_priming.py} using the same
\texttt{paper\_main} transitive preset, differing only in the model name and
output directory. For each item and target structure, the model scores the same
target under a congruent prime and an incongruent prime.

Token-level priming effect:
\[
\mathrm{PE}_{token} = \log P(t_i \mid \text{prime}_{congruent}) - \log P(t_i \mid \text{prime}_{incongruent}).
\]

Aggregations:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \texttt{sentence\_pe}: sum of token PE across the target sentence.
    \item \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean}: mean token PE across the target sentence.
    \item \texttt{critical\_word\_pe} and \texttt{critical\_word\_pe\_mean}:
    summed and token-normalized PE at target word index 2.
\end{itemize}

The primary metric in this draft is \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean}. We chose it
because raw \texttt{sentence\_pe} is strongly affected by tokenizer-specific
length differences, which are especially substantial in strict Jabberwocky.
Mean token PE provides the most stable cross-model comparison among the metrics
currently implemented in the repository.

\subsection*{Statistical analysis}

Statistics were computed with
\texttt{scripts/Phase-1/5\_analyze\_transitive\_statistics.py} for each model
separately. The main inferential unit was the paired passive-minus-active
contrast at item level within each condition.

We used:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item sign-flip permutation tests (\(n=10{,}000\)),
    \item bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals (\(n=10{,}000\)),
    \item one-sample \(t\)-tests and paired \(d_z\),
    \item a confound-aware regression on paired differences with HC3 robust
    standard errors,
    \item and per-condition linear mixed-effects models with random intercept by
    item.
\end{itemize}

The paired-difference regression was:
\[
\Delta \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean} \sim \texttt{condition}
+ z(\Delta \texttt{target\_length})
+ z(\Delta \texttt{critical\_word\_token\_count}).
\]

The per-condition mixed-effects robustness model was:
\[
\texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean} \sim \texttt{target\_structure}
+ z(\texttt{target\_length})
+ z(\texttt{critical\_word\_token\_count})
+ (1 \mid \texttt{item}).
\]

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Experiment 1: \texttt{gpt2-large}}

Table~\ref{tab:gpt2desc} reports descriptive means for the original
\texttt{gpt2-large} run. Positive values indicate higher target probability
after congruent primes.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Descriptive priming means for \texttt{gpt2-large}.}
\label{tab:gpt2desc}
\begin{tabular}{llrrrr}
\toprule
Condition & Target & \texttt{sentence\_pe} & \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean} & \texttt{critical\_word\_pe\_mean} & \(n\) \\
\midrule
CORE & active & 0.1907 & 0.0312 & 0.1304 & 15000 \\
CORE & passive & 1.3985 & 0.1746 & 0.1757 & 15000 \\
ANOMALOUS & active & -0.3384 & -0.0559 & 0.1115 & 15000 \\
ANOMALOUS & passive & 1.5548 & 0.1940 & 0.1597 & 15000 \\
jabberwocky & active & -1.8562 & -0.1121 & 0.0447 & 15000 \\
jabberwocky & passive & 7.1376 & 0.3854 & 0.8082 & 15000 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For the primary metric (\texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean}), passive-minus-active
differences were positive in all conditions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item CORE: \(+0.1434\), 95\% CI \([0.1375, 0.1493]\), \(d_z=0.389\)
    \item ANOMALOUS: \(+0.2500\), 95\% CI \([0.2433, 0.2566]\), \(d_z=0.594\)
    \item jabberwocky: \(+0.4975\), 95\% CI \([0.4921, 0.5029]\), \(d_z=1.482\)
\end{itemize}

All sign-flip permutation tests reached the minimum attainable value with
10,000 permutations (\(p=0.0001\)).

The per-condition LMM passive coefficients were:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item CORE: \(\beta=0.1704\), SE \(=0.0270\), \(p=2.96\times10^{-10}\)
    \item ANOMALOUS: \(\beta=0.4098\), SE \(=0.0301\), \(p=4.01\times10^{-42}\)
    \item jabberwocky: \(\beta=0.6497\), SE \(=0.0149\), \(p\approx 0\)
\end{itemize}

In the paired-difference regression, \(z(\Delta\texttt{target\_length})\) was
not significant (\(p=0.88\)), while
\(z(\Delta\texttt{critical\_word\_token\_count})\) was positive and significant
(\(\beta=0.0884,\ p=1.58\times10^{-19}\)). Thus, for
\texttt{gpt2-large}, critical-word tokenization asymmetry contributed to effect
magnitude but did not remove the overall passive asymmetry.

\subsection*{Experiment 2: \texttt{pythia-410m}}

We repeated the same analysis pipeline with \texttt{pythia-410m}. In the raw
output tables, the same Jabberwocky corpus appears under the label
\texttt{jabberwocky-only}; this is a naming artifact of the replication run, not
a different stimulus set.

Table~\ref{tab:pythiadesc} reports descriptive means for the replication.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Descriptive priming means for \texttt{pythia-410m}.}
\label{tab:pythiadesc}
\begin{tabular}{llrrrr}
\toprule
Condition & Target & \texttt{sentence\_pe} & \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean} & \texttt{critical\_word\_pe\_mean} & \(n\) \\
\midrule
CORE & active & 0.0671 & 0.0103 & 0.2799 & 15000 \\
CORE & passive & 1.3342 & 0.1664 & 0.0869 & 15000 \\
ANOMALOUS & active & 0.1130 & 0.0181 & 0.1778 & 15000 \\
ANOMALOUS & passive & 0.8960 & 0.1117 & 0.0581 & 15000 \\
jabberwocky & active & -1.6320 & -0.0997 & 0.0312 & 15000 \\
jabberwocky & passive & 5.4393 & 0.2967 & 0.5275 & 15000 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For the primary metric (\texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean}), passive-minus-active
differences again remained positive in all conditions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item CORE: \(+0.1561\), 95\% CI \([0.1492, 0.1628]\), \(d_z=0.365\)
    \item ANOMALOUS: \(+0.0936\), 95\% CI \([0.0864, 0.1008]\), \(d_z=0.206\)
    \item jabberwocky: \(+0.3964\), 95\% CI \([0.3902, 0.4025]\), \(d_z=1.045\)
\end{itemize}

All sign-flip permutation tests again reached the minimum value with 10,000
permutations (\(p=0.0001\)).

The mixed-effects robustness pattern was weaker than in
\texttt{gpt2-large}. The per-condition LMM passive coefficients were:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item CORE: \(\beta=-0.0588\), SE \(=0.0341\), \(p=0.0848\)
    \item ANOMALOUS: \(\beta=0.0405\), SE \(=0.0283\), \(p=0.1532\)
    \item jabberwocky: \(\beta=0.2224\), SE \(=0.0133\), \(p=1.26\times10^{-62}\)
\end{itemize}

Thus, \texttt{pythia-410m} robustly reproduced the passive advantage only in
Jabberwocky once target length and critical-word token-count covariates were
included in the mixed-effects model.

The paired-difference regression also differed from \texttt{gpt2-large}. For
\texttt{pythia-410m}, \(z(\Delta\texttt{target\_length})\) was positive and
significant (\(\beta=0.0194,\ p=4.53\times10^{-19}\)), while
\(z(\Delta\texttt{critical\_word\_token\_count})\) was not significant
(\(p=0.71\)). This suggests that in the Pythia replication, length asymmetry was
the more important nuisance factor, whereas critical-word tokenization asymmetry
was less explanatory than in \texttt{gpt2-large}.

\subsection*{Comparative interpretation}

The cross-model comparison yields three main observations.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item Both models show a positive passive-minus-active effect on the primary
    item-paired metric in all three conditions, with the largest effect in
    Jabberwocky.
    \item \texttt{gpt2-large} shows a stronger and more uniform passive bias:
    \texttt{sentence\_pe\_mean} passive-minus-active is \(0.1434/0.2500/0.4975\)
    for CORE/ANOMALOUS/Jabberwocky, compared with
    \(0.1561/0.0936/0.3964\) for \texttt{pythia-410m}.
    \item The mixed-effects robustness analysis separates the models more
    sharply. For \texttt{gpt2-large}, the passive coefficient is positive and
    highly reliable in all three conditions. For \texttt{pythia-410m}, the same
    coefficient is clearly positive only in Jabberwocky and is small or
    non-significant in CORE and ANOMALOUS.
\end{itemize}

Taken together, the replication supports the existence of a passive-favoring
priming signal beyond a single model, but also suggests that the signal is not
equally stable across model families. The cross-model convergence is strongest
for Jabberwocky, which is also the condition most directly aligned with the
current theoretical goal of testing morphosyntactic persistence under reduced
lexical semantics.
